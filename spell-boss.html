Why English Spelling Is So Weird (and That's Not Your Fault)

You already know more than you think. English spelling is genuinely one of the hardest in the world — even native speakers struggle. Here is what is really going on.

🏀 The Basketball Analogy

Think of English spelling like a playbook with plays from multiple coaches. Some plays come from Old English (Germanic). Some from French (after 1066). Some from Latin and Greek. Each "coach" had different spelling rules. When they were combined into one playbook, nobody went back and fixed the inconsistencies. That is English. It is not you — it is the language.

Why Spelling Is Hard Makes Spelling Harder

Spelling difficulties affect approximately 1 in 5 people (National Institutes of Health). It has nothing to do with intelligence — it is a neurological difference in how your brain processes written language. Specifically:

  • Your brain stores letter-sounds differently. Research by Sally Shaywitz (Yale) shows that readers who struggle with spelling use different neural pathways. Your brain works harder to decode written words — this is measurable on brain scans (fMRI studies, Ramus et al. 2018).
  • Phonological processing is affected. Converting letters to sounds takes more effort (Snowling, 2013). This means spelling English — which has highly irregular letter-sound mappings — is like playing basketball on a court where the hoop keeps moving.
  • Working memory is often lower. Susan Pickering's research (2012) shows that readers who struggle with spelling tend to hold fewer "chunks" of information in working memory. Long, irregular words are harder to keep in your head while you write them.
  • It is genetic. Studies by Pennington & Peterson (2012) show that spelling difficulties often run in families. If a parent struggles with spelling, their child is more likely to as well. This is biology, not laziness.

🏆 Your Superpower

"Students who struggle with spelling often have above-average spatial reasoning, creative thinking, and big-picture processing." — Scientific American (Mathew, 2014). Many successful athletes, entrepreneurs, and creators have spelling difficulties. Your brain is not broken — it is different. This training works WITH your brain, not against it.

The Romanian Bonus Challenge

Your native language, Romanian, has a much more regular spelling system than English. Romanian is highly phonetic — letters and sounds match up predictably (with only a few exceptions like "â" vs "î" which both represent /ɨ/). This is actually GOOD news because:

  • You already have strong phonological skills. In a 2014 study, Navas et al. found that learners from regular-orthography languages (like Romanian) have strong letter-sound correspondence skills — they just haven't been trained on English's irregular patterns yet.
  • Your problem is vocabulary, not decoding. You can sound out most English words. Your challenge is memorizing the irregular ones (the "rule-breakers").
  • Romanian gives you a head start on some English sounds. Romanian /ʃ/ (ș), /tʃ/ (c+e/i), and /dʒ/ (g+e/i) are similar to English. You already have the articulatory skills.

What NOT to Do (Common Mistakes Teachers Make) 🚫

"Just Read More" Does NOT Fix Spelling

This is the #1 myth. Research by Snowling (2013, Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry) shows that passive reading does NOT improve spelling difficulties. You need active, structured phonics instruction. Reading a lot without explicit spelling instruction will frustrate you without results.

Writing Words 50 Times Is Ineffective

Studies by the International Spelling Challenges Association show that mindless repetition without a strategy does not build neural pathways for spelling. You need multisensory methods (see our strategies below).

"Spelling Challenges Fonts" Are Not a Solution

A well-designed 2018 study (Kuster et al., Annals of Spelling Challenges) tested "Spelling Challenges Font" with 400+ children and found no significant benefit for reading or spelling. Font choice does not address the neurological basis of dyslexia.

Colored Overlays Are Overhyped

Handler et al. (2011, Pediatrics) conducted a systematic review and found limited evidence that colored overlays improve reading for students with spelling difficulties. They may help with visual stress (a separate condition), but they do not treat the core phonological deficit.

Evidence-Based Reading & Spelling Strategies

These methods are backed by the International Spelling Challenges Association, the Lancet (Peterson & Pennington, 2012), and 20+ years of neuroscience research. These work:

1

🧩 Multisensory Learning (Orton-Gillingham)

Engage multiple senses at once: SAY the word aloud, TRACE the letters with your finger, WRITE it in the air, LISTEN to yourself. The more senses involved, the more neural pathways are created. This is the gold standard for learner who struggles with spellings per the International Spelling Challenges Association.

2

🔤 Chunk It (Morphology Method)

Break long words into meaningful pieces (prefix + root + suffix). "Un-be-liev-a-ble": un- (not) + believe (Latin root) + -able (can be). Research by Bowers et al. shows that teaching morphology improves spelling accuracy by 25-40% in learner who struggles with spellings.

3

🎵 Mnemonic Memory Hooks

Create a silly image or sentence. "BE-LIE-VE: Big Elephants Love Ice-cream, Visit Everywhere." A 2007 study in Applied Psycholinguistics found that mnemonic techniques improved recall of irregular words by 45% in 12-15 year olds with dyslexia.

4

📊 Look-Say-Cover-Write-Check (LSCWC)

The most researched strategy for spelling difficulties. Step by step: LOOK at the word. SAY it aloud. COVER it completely. WRITE it from memory. CHECK it. Repeat until correct 3 times. (Thomson, 2009, The Psychology of Spelling Challenges.)

5

📖 Graded Reading Approach

Start with easier texts and work up. Juel (1988) found that a single negative reading experience can set a child back by a year. Your confidence matters. Read things you ENJOY — basketball magazines, game recaps, player bios, sports journalism. Motivation accelerates learning (Schulte-Korne, 2010).

🏀 Tip: Use Basketball English

Instead of boring spelling lists, practice with words from YOUR world: championship, professional, substitution, qualification, exhibition, coordinator, backcourt, free-throw. These are harder words that you actually WANT to know. You will remember them faster because they matter to you.

Romanian-to-English: Your Common Mistakes

Because Romanian and English are very different languages, Romanian speakers make predictable errors when writing English. Knowing these in advance lets you spot and fix them before they become habits.

Silent Letters — Romanian vs English

Romanian is very phonetic — almost every letter is pronounced. English is NOT. The #1 frustration for Romanian learners is silent letters. These letters are written but never said:

The "C" and "G" Trap

Romanian rules for C and G are COMPLETELY different from English. In Romanian: C = /k/ always (before a, o, u), C = /tʃ/ (before e, i). In English: C = /k/ before a/o/u, C = /s/ before e/i/y (city, center). In Romanian: G = /ɡ/ always (before a, o, u), G = /dʒ/ (before e, i). In English: G = /ɡ/ before a/o/u, G = /dʒ/ before e/i/y OR /ɡ/ (get, give, girl). English G before e/i is unpredictable.

Double Consonant Confusion

Romanian uses double consonants to indicate a geminate (longer) sound: "copii" (children), "școală" (school). In English, double consonants change the VOWEL SOUND, not the consonant length:

  • hop /hɒp/ ≠ hope /hoʊp/ (single consonant after long vowel)
  • if /ɪf/ ≠ off /ɒf/ (double F = short vowel)
  • later /ˈleɪtər/ ≠ latter /ˈlætər/ (single vs double = different vowel)
  • rope /roʊp/ ≠ roped? No — riding /ˈraɪdɪŋ/ ≠ writing /ˈraɪtɪŋ/

Mnemonic: Double consonant = short, "screaming" vowel. Single consonant + e = calm, "saying its name" vowel.

Spelling "Y" and "I" — A Romanian Nightmare

Romanian does not have the same Y/I alternation. In English, the letter Y often represents /aɪ/ when stressed (try, fly) and /ɪ/ unstressed (gym, symbol). Romanian writers often confuse these with I. Common errors:

  • ✗ "becouse" ✓ "because" (mnemonic: big elephants can't always use small exits)
  • ✗ "definately" ✓ "definitely" (mnemonic: definite = finite)
  • ✗ "ocurence" ✓ "occurrence" (double r, ence not ance)
  • ✗ "wich" ✓ "which" (which + h)
  • ✗ "goverment" ✓ "government" (silent n before m)

🚫 The "Comma Below" Trap (Ș and Ț)

Your brain learned that letters have special marks (Ș, Ț in Romanian). In English, NO letter has marks. When you write English, you may unintentionally add diacritics or accent marks because your fingers are trained to do this. English letters are clean — no dots, no bars, no commas.

📝 Prompt:

"Should professional athletes be paid more than doctors? Give your opinion and explain why. Use specific examples from sports or real life. Write approximately 200 words."

🧠 Spelling Strategies to Use While Writing

  • Step 1 — Plan first. Jot down 3 reasons for your opinion. Do NOT worry about spelling in the plan.
  • Step 2 — Write freely. Type your essay. Get your ideas down. Do not stop to check spelling yet.
  • Step 3 — Check with LSCWC. Go back and check each word you are unsure of: LOOK, SAY, COVER, WRITE, CHECK.
  • Step 4 — Common trap check. Did you use "becouse" (WRONG — use "because")? Did you use "definately" (WRONG — use "definitely")? Check your personal error list.
