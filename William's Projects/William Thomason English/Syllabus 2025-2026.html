English Language Programme

A structured approach to confident communication

02

Lesson Details

Each unit follows a consistent session structure: warm-up, main focus, interactive activity, and reflection.

Unit 1 — Descriptive Language

April · Adjectives, prepositions, there is/are
01

Build vivid descriptions using sensory details, spatial language, and clear structure. Learners practise describing images, scenes, and personal spaces aloud.

Speaking Focus

Describe a picture (event, place, or daily scene) for 1–2 minutes using full sentences.

Activity

Picture Chain — pass an image around; each person adds one descriptive sentence.

Unit 2 — Present Perfect Continuous

May · Actions that started in the past and continue
02

Talk about ongoing activities, habits, and experiences with time expressions. Focus on the difference between duration and completion.

Speaking Focus

"How long have you been...?" pair interviews about hobbies, studies, and interests.

Activity

Club interviews — share one interesting fact about a partner with the group.

Unit 3 — Reported Speech

June · Reporting what others say
03

Learn to relay conversations, share opinions, and report news accurately. Covers tense shifts and reporting verbs in a conversational context.

Speaking Focus

"What did your friend say about...?" — practise retelling real or imagined conversations.

Activity

Role-play a phone call, then report it to the class: "She told me that..."

Unit 4 — Passive Voice

July · Focusing on the action, not the actor
04

Shift focus from the doer to the action. Particularly useful for describing processes, traditions, and how things work in the real world.

Speaking Focus

Describe how everyday things are made or done: food, crafts, local customs, school rules.

Activity

Photo descriptions using passive: "This temple was built in..." / "Rice is grown in..."

Unit 5 — Mid-Year Review

August · Consolidation and confidence check
05

A chance to revisit the first four units through games, error correction, and student-led mini-talks. Identifies strengths and areas to develop in the second half.

Activities

Review quiz with grammar cards, board games, and short student presentations on any topic from Units 1–4.

Unit 6 — Conditionals

September · Real situations and imaginary scenarios
06

From everyday truths to wild hypotheticals. Zero, first, and second conditionals taught through scenario-based speaking rather than drill.

Speaking Focus

"What will you do if...?" (real) and "What would you do if you were...?" (imaginary).

Activity

Interview game: "If you could meet anyone, who would it be? What would you ask them?"

Unit 7 — Idioms & Expressions

October · Natural, everyday English
07

Common idioms and phrasal verbs that native speakers use constantly. Focus on understanding and using them in context, not memorising lists.

Speaking Focus

Create short skits or stories that naturally incorporate target idioms.

Activity

Idiom matching + charades. "Idom of the Day" mini-presentations.

Unit 8 — Emails & Messages

November · Formal vs. informal register
08

Navigate the difference between writing to a friend, a teacher, or a company. Practical communication skills for the real world.

Speaking Focus

Read a message aloud, then role-play a spoken response. Practise tone and register.

Activity

Write and act out a reply to a sample email or invitation.

Unit 9 — Describing a Process

December · Sequencing and instructions
09

Explain how something is made or done — from recipes to daily routines to cultural events. Sequencing language is the backbone.

Speaking Focus

"How to..." — give step-by-step instructions for a real process you know well.

Activity

Short live demonstration with spoken commentary: a recipe, a game, a craft.

Unit 10 — Revision & Prep

January · Bringing it all together
10

Consolidate the full year's grammar. Focus on picture description fluency and begin planning the end-of-year presentation project.

Activities

Extended picture description practice, project outlining, peer feedback sessions.

Unit 11 — Year-End Presentation

February · "My Dream Future" — speak 2–3 minutes
11

The capstone project. Learners deliver a 2–3 minute presentation using grammar from the entire year: present, past, future, conditionals, passive, reported speech, and descriptive language.

Final Presentation

Peer feedback via Compliment Circle. Visual supports (slides or drawings) encouraged but not required.

03

Final Presentation Guide

Four slides. Every grammar point from the year. Speak for 2–3 minutes with confidence.

Slide 1 — My Dream Place

Where? Why?

Describe a real or imagined location. Use present and future tenses, vivid adjectives, and sensory detail.

Example: "My ideal home is in a quiet city because I love being close to nature. It has large windows and a garden."
Slide 2 — Daily Life

What does it look like? What happens there?

Describe rooms, routines, and surroundings. Use adjectives, passive voice, and present continuous.

Example: "The living room is decorated with art from my travels. People are always sharing meals together."
Slide 3 — What I Can Do There

Hobbies, goals, adventures

Talk about possibilities and imagined experiences. Use modals (can, could) and conditionals.

Example: "If I lived there, I would explore the coast every weekend. You can learn to cook local dishes."
Slide 4 — Who & Why It Matters

The people. The meaning.

Describe relationships, role models, and personal significance. Use present perfect, reported speech, and descriptive language.

Example: "I have been dreaming about this for years. My best friend said that we should make it happen together."
04

Session Methodology

Every session follows the same predictable flow. Familiarity breeds confidence.

1

Warm-up

10–15 minutes of low-pressure speaking. Quick questions, pair-share, or picture description. Topics from daily life: family, hobbies, school, friends.

2

Main Focus

Grammar and vocabulary introduced in context, immediately practised through structured speaking tasks tied to the unit topic.

3

Interactive Activity

Games, role-plays, or video-based tasks. Dice games, charades, interviews, picture chains. The goal is spontaneous, joyful use of the target language.

4

Wrap-up

Brief reflection or mini-presentation. Students share what they learned, what they found challenging, or what they want to practise next time.

05

Additional Resources

Supporting materials that extend learning beyond the session.

Conversation Practice

Curated question lists (300+ topics) for warm-ups and free discussion. Organised by theme: identity, daily life, opinions, future plans.

Video Content

Short ESL clips, explainer videos, and fun content from YouTube. Always tied to the unit's grammar or topic focus.

Games & Activities

Story dice, charades, board games with grammar cards, picture chains, interview grids. Designed for pairs and small groups.

Cultural Tie-ins

Topics drawn from the learner's own context and interests — local culture, festivals, current events, shared media. Relevance drives engagement.

Ready to begin?

This syllabus is a living document. Each unit can be adapted to the learner's level, interests, and pace. The goal is always the same: genuine communication, not just correct answers.
