🔊 What Causes Feedback?

Feedback happens when your guitar's pickup or microphone picks up sound from the stage monitors or PA system, creating a loop: guitar → pickup → amp/monitor → guitar → pickup → SCREECH! In big halls with powerful PA systems, this is even more likely because there's more sound energy bouncing around the room.

The Golden Rules
Do this, not that

✅ DO

  • Face AWAY from stage monitors
  • Use a notch/feedback eliminator pedal
  • Cut the problematic frequencies on EQ
  • Use a soundhole cover to reduce resonance
  • Keep your guitar volume moderate — let the PA do the work
  • Ask the sound engineer for a monitor check before the show
  • Use a directional (cardioid) microphone if miking your guitar
  • Position monitors at 45° angles behind you, not directly facing you

❌ DON'T

  • Stand directly in front of a monitor
  • Crank your guitar volume to maximum
  • Point your guitar's soundhole at the monitors
  • Ignore the soundcheck — always test before the audience arrives
  • Use an omnidirectional mic that picks up everything
  • Place your amp or monitor behind you aimed at the guitar's pickup
  • Forget to cut the feedback frequency once you find it
  • Assume the sound engineer knows your instrument's feedback points

6 Key Techniques to Prevent Feedback
Master these and you'll never fear the stage again
1

🎛️ EQ — Cut the Feedback Frequencies

Every guitar and room has specific frequencies that feed back first. During soundcheck, slowly raise the volume until feedback starts, then use a parametric EQ to cut that frequency by 3-6 dB. Common feedback zones: 800 Hz, 1.2 kHz, 2.5 kHz, 4 kHz.

💡 Pro tip: Use a graphic EQ pedal on your guitar to cut problem frequencies before they reach the PA.
2

🔇 Use a Soundhole Cover

A feedback buster (rubber or plastic disc) that fits into your guitar's soundhole dramatically reduces internal resonance and feedback. It's the single most effective physical fix for acoustic guitars. Brands: Feedback Buster, Superplug.

💡 This alone can eliminate 80% of feedback problems. Always carry one in your gig bag.
3

🧭 Position Yourself Correctly

Your guitar's pickup has a "hot spot" — the direction it's most sensitive to incoming sound. Face away from monitors and angle your guitar so the back (not the soundhole) faces the speakers. In big halls, stay at least 10 feet from any monitor wedge.

💡 If you must face the audience (and therefore the monitors behind you), angle your guitar slightly to the side.
4

🎚️ Volume Management

The #1 mistake: too much guitar volume on stage. In a big hall with a PA, your guitar only needs to be loud enough for YOU to hear. Let the front-of-house engineer handle the audience volume. Keep your onboard preamp at 60-70% max.

💡 If you can't hear yourself, ask for more monitor mix — don't turn up your guitar.
5

🎤 Mic Technique (If Using a Mic)

If you're using a microphone instead of a pickup: use a cardioid pattern mic (rejects sound from behind), keep the mic close to the guitar (6-8 inches), and aim the mic's null point at the monitors. Never use an omnidirectional mic on stage.

💡 A clip-on condenser mic (like DPA 4099) is more feedback-resistant than a stand mic.
6

🔧 Feedback Eliminator Pedal

Devices like the Boss FB-2, Feedback Ferret, or LR Baggs Align automatically detect and notch out feedback frequencies in real-time. Set it to "auto" mode and it will find and kill feedback before you hear it.

💡 The LR Baggs Align Reverb has a built-in feedback suppressor — great for acoustic performers.
