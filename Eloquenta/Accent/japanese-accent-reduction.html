The Core Challenge

Why Japanese Speakers Struggle with English

First-language (L1) phonological transfer — the science

Japanese Phonology
5 vowels. 1 consonant liquid (the alveolar tap [ɾ]). Moraic syllable timing. Pitch accent — not stress accent. Devoicing of /i u/ between voiceless consonants. No syllable-final consonants.
Vance (1987); Japanese Phonology (Wikipedia)
English Phonology
14+ vowels. 2 liquids (/l/ and /r/). Stress-timed rhythm. Contrastive voicing. Syllable-final consonants. No moraic structure. Schwa reduction in unstressed syllables.
Non-native Pronunciations of English (Wikipedia)
THE BOTTLENECK
Japanese has one liquid phoneme /r/ (realized as [ɾ] or sometimes [l]). English has two — rhotic /r/ and lateral /l/. Goto (1971) found that adult Japanese speakers cannot reliably perceive the acoustic difference between them. No perception → no production.

Forming /r/ and /l/ Correctly

Practical articulatory training — what to do with your tongue

English /r/ — Postalveolar Approximant [ɹ]
1. Curl the tip of your tongue BACK toward the roof — but do not touch the alveolar ridge.

2. Slightly round your lips (think "oo" shape).

3. The sides of your tongue touch your upper molars. The tip stays suspended in the air.

Drill: Say "rrrrr" — if your tongue tips forward and taps the ridge, you're making Japanese [ɾ]. Curl back. Lift the tip off the ridge.
English /l/ — Alveolar Lateral [l]
1. Press the tip of your tongue firmly AGAINST the alveolar ridge (bumpy area behind upper front teeth).

2. Keep the tip pressed while letting air flow around the sides of the tongue ("lateral" air).

3. Do NOT curl the tongue back. Keep it flat and forward.

Drill: Say "lllll" and slowly curl the tongue back — you'll hear /l/ degrade into /r/. The curl-back is exactly what causes the confusion. Prevent it.
THE KEY INSIGHT
The two sounds are nearly identical in tongue POSITION — the difference is 5mm of tip placement and whether it touches or hovers. This is invisible to Japanese ears. Articulatory training, not just listening, is what works (Goto 1971; McClelland, Fiez & McCandliss 2002).

What the Research Says

Landmark Studies on R/L Acquisition

50 years of empirical evidence

Goto (1971) — The Foundation
Adult Japanese L2 learners cannot perceive the /r/-/l/ acoustic difference — yet some learned to produce it through articulatory training alone, without hearing it. Perception and production can be trained separately.
Neuropsychologia, 9(3), 317-323
Miyawaki et al. (1975) — The Surprise
When /r/ and /l/ were acoustically manipulated to sound less like speech (formant removal), Japanese speakers could distinguish them as well as native English speakers. The problem is phonological processing, not auditory ability.
Perception & Psychophysics, 18(5), 331-340
Bradlow et al. (1997) — Transfer
Perceptual /r/-/l/ training transferred to improved production — but with wide individual variation. Some learners improved dramatically; others showed minimal production gains despite perceptual progress. Production drills are still needed.
JASA, 101(4), 2299-2310
Takagi & Mann (1995) — The Limit
Even Japanese speakers with 12+ years in the US still identify /r/ and /l/ less accurately than natives. Extended naturalistic exposure alone is not sufficient. Structured training is required.
Applied Psycholinguistics, 16(4), 379-405

For Teachers

Teaching Accent Reduction: A Teacher's Guide

How to run effective sessions with Japanese business professionals

1
Diagnose Before You Teach
Record a 2-minute spontaneous speaking sample (client meeting role-play). Identify the student's specific error pattern: Is it R/L? TH? Vowels? Epenthesis? Prosody? Every Japanese learner has a different profile. Don't use a one-size-fits-all curriculum.
💡 Tip: Use the 9-question diagnostic at slide 15. Takes 10 minutes and gives you a precise starting point.
2
Separate Perception from Production
Don't jump to production drills before the student can hear the difference. Spend session 1 on perception: minimal pair identification with feedback. Use 5+ speaker recordings (Lively et al. 1993). The 7-accent audio player on slide 7 is your primary tool here.
💡 Tip: If a student can't hear the /r/-/l/ difference after 3 sessions, switch to articulatory-only training (Goto 1971 method). It works without perception.
3
Use the Mirror — Always
Articulatory training requires visual feedback. Every student must see their own tongue position. For /r/: tongue tip curled back, not touching. For /l/: tip pressed to ridge. For TH: tongue between teeth. For F/V: upper teeth on lower lip. Group classes need a mirror per student or phone selfie camera.
💡 Tip: Have students place a finger lightly on their chin. For /r/, the chin drops slightly. For /l/, it stays still. Kinesthetic feedback accelerates learning.
4
Prioritise by Business Impact
Not all errors are equal. In client-facing roles, the priority order is: (1) Eliminate epenthesis — "risuku" damages intelligibility instantly. (2) Fix vowel reduction — schwa is the #1 predictor of foreign accent (Flege et al. 1996). (3) Train R/L — visible, embarrassing errors. (4) Stress and prosody — the hidden accent that exhausts listeners.
💡 Tip: Use the student's actual client emails and meeting transcripts as drilling material. Authentic content > generic textbook sentences.
5
Record, Compare, Repeat
Every session: record the student on Day 1 phrases. Re-record in Weeks 2, 4, 6. Play both recordings side by side. This is the most powerful motivator — students hear their own improvement. It also provides objective data for the "measuring progress" component (slide 20).
💡 Tip: Use a consistent 10-phrase benchmark set each time: "I think we should review the contract. Let's schedule a call with our partners in London. The next step is to finalize our proposal."

Teaching Accent Reduction: Scripts & Drills

Ready-to-use classroom scripts for teachers

Warm-Up Script (5 min)
"Today we're going to retrain how your tongue makes two sounds. Watch my mouth — [demonstrate /r/ vs /l/ slowly]. Put your hand under your chin. For R, your tongue pulls back and your chin drops slightly. For L, your tongue presses forward and up. Try it — [l]ight... [r]ight... again..."

Before any drill, do 20 repetitions with mouth open and tongue visible. Slow-motion first.
Perception Drill Script
"You'll hear two words. Tell me: same or different. [Play 'light' then 'right'] — different. [Play 'light' then 'light'] — same. You get 10 rounds. I'll go faster each time."

Use the 7-accent player. Start with USA/UK (most difference), end with accents where /l/ and /r/ are closer (SC, IRE).
Epenthesis Drill Script
"Japanese wants to add a vowel after every consonant. English doesn't. Say 'next' — one syllable. Don't say 'nekusuto'. [Student tries]. Good. Now 'risk' — one syllable. Don't say 'risuku'. Let's count: nek-su-to = 3 syllables = wrong. Next = 1 = right."

Practice: text, script, product, strength, strict. The harder the cluster, the more satisfying the success.
Prosody Script
"In English, important words are LOUDER and LONGER. Listen: 'we need to RE-view the con-TRACT.' Two words in caps — those get stress. Now try it. Make RE and CONTRACT louder. [Student tries]. Yes! The other words — 'we need to' — say them fast and quiet, like [schwa sounds]."

Beyond R/L

TH, F/V, and Syllable-Final Consonants

Three high-frequency business pronunciation challenges

/θ/ /ð/ — "TH" Sounds
Japanese lacks dental fricatives. "Think" → "sink", "this" → "zis", "through" → "suru".
Drill: Bite tongue tip gently. Blow air. Mirror check. "I think this is the right path through the data."
/f/ /v/
/f/ → bilabial [ɸ] (blow through lips, not teeth). /v/ → [b] (no voicing distinction without teeth-lip contact).
Drill: Bite lower lip with upper teeth. "We have five viable alternatives." Feel teeth-lip contact on every /f/ and /v/.
Vowel Epenthesis
Vowel insertion after consonants: "product" → "purodakuto", "risk" → "risuku". Destroys English rhythm entirely.
Drill: Consonant clusters without vowels. "Sixths" [sɪksθs] — one syllable, five consonants. Feel each consonant connect without a vowel gap between them.

Rhythm, Stress and Prosody

The hidden accent — why Japanese English sounds "flat" to English ears

Mora-Timed → Stress-Timed
Japanese is mora-timed — equal syllable duration. English is stress-timed — content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) are louder, longer, higher-pitched; function words (the, of, to) are reduced to schwa or lost entirely.
Pitch Accent → Stress Accent
Japanese uses pitch to define words (hashi = chopsticks vs bridge). English uses loudness and length (REcord vs reCORD). Japanese speakers apply pitch where English expects stress, sounding monotone.
WHY THIS MATTERS IN BUSINESS
Stressed syllables carry information and emphasis. "We need to reVIEW the conTRACT" — stress shows which words matter. Without it, English listeners report fatigue. In a pitch: "We have a proPOSAL from our partNER" — lost stress = lost impact.

Schwa and Vowel Reduction

The single biggest predictor of perceived foreign accent

The Schwa /ə/
English unstressed syllables reduce to a neutral [ə]. Japanese speakers pronounce the written vowel: "decoration" → [dekoɾeːɕoɴ] with full vowels instead of [dekəreɪʃn].
This is the #1 predictor of perceived foreign accent — not consonants (Flege et al. 1996).
5 Vowels vs 14+
Japanese 5 monophthongs collapse English vowels: /æ/ and /ʌ/ → /a/, /ɪ/ and /iː/ → /i/, /ʊ/ and /uː/ → /uː/. Pairs to master: trap [æ] vs cup [ʌ]; ship [ɪ] vs sheep [iː]; pull [ʊ] vs pool [uː].

The Program

8-Week Intensive — Course Outline

Prioritised by research-backed impact on intelligibility

Weeks 1-2: Perception Training
High-variability minimal pair training (Lively et al. 1994 protocol). 5 speakers, feedback, multiple sessions. Focus: /r/-/l/, /θ/-/s/, /v/-/b/, vowel pairs. Build accurate perception before production.
Weeks 3-4: Articulatory Foundation
Physical placement training with mirror. Tongue position drills. Lip rounding for /r/. Teeth-lip contact for /f v/. TH with tongue between teeth. Epenthesis elimination: consonant cluster practice.
Weeks 5-6: Prosody and Rhythm
Stress-timed rhythm drills. Content vs function word exercises. Schwa reduction practice. Sentence-level emphasis. Business phrase drilling: "Let me clarify our position on this matter."
Weeks 7-8: Business Application
Client meeting role-plays with accent focus. Presentation rehearsal with recording and review. Email-to-speech: reading business documents aloud. Self-assessment with before/after recordings.
Protocol informed by: Lively et al. (1993, 1994); Bradlow et al. (1997); Goto (1971); McClelland et al. (2002); Guion et al. (2000)

Measuring Progress

Objective benchmarks — not subjective impressions

Acoustic Analysis
Track F2/F3 formant values for /r/ and /l/ (Kuzniak & Zapf 2004). Compare to native speaker ranges. Measure progress in Hertz, not feelings.
Native Listener Rating
Native English speakers rate recordings on 9-point scale (1 = heavy accent, 9 = native-like). Use multiple listeners to average out bias.
Communicative Intelligibility
Dictation tests: native listeners transcribe what they hear. Target: >90% word accuracy in controlled conversation. This is the metric that matters in business.
EXPECTED OUTCOMES
Lively et al. (1994): significant improvement after just 3 weeks of targeted training. Takagi & Mann (1995): 12 years in-country without training still falls short of native performance. Structured training delivers results that natural exposure cannot. For a business consultant, every intelligibility gain = credibility in client interactions.

The Summary

Six Evidence-Based Principles

For Japanese business English improvement

1 · R/L Is Phonological, Not Auditory
The problem isn't hearing — it's categorical perception. Miyawaki et al. (1975): Japanese ears CAN hear the difference acoustically. The brain maps both to one category. Training creates two new categories.
2 · Production Needs Articulatory Training
Goto (1971): You can produce /r/ and /l/ through physical placement alone — even without perceiving the difference. Mirror and tongue-position drills are essential.
3 · Vowels Matter More Than Consonants
Schwa reduction and vowel quality account for more perceived accent than any consonant (Flege et al. 1996). The 5-vowel vs 14+ gap must be explicitly trained.
4 · Epenthesis Is the Intelligibility Killer
"Nekusuto" instead of "next." Added vowels break word recognition. Eliminating epenthesis gives the fastest intelligibility return of any single intervention.
5 · Training Beats Exposure
12 years in-country ≠ native-like R/L (Takagi & Mann 1995). 3 weeks of targeted training = significant gains (Lively et al. 1994). Method matters more than duration.
6 · Prosody Is Business Critical
Consonant accuracy without stress still sounds foreign. Prosody carries information, emphasis, and engagement. In client meetings, how you sound = how you're perceived.
